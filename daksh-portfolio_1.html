Who I am

A student asking inconvenient questions about water

I'm Daksh, a student at Oakridge International School, Hyderabad. I became obsessed with one problem: small and marginal farmers in Telangana irrigate by gut feeling, wasting up to 40% of water while 89% of India's irrigation remains flood-based.

I designed and ran my own smart irrigation experiments — deploying sensors in real fields and using evapotranspiration models to prove what intuition couldn't. That work became PANI: a sensor-validated, AI-driven irrigation framework, entirely my own.

Alongside this, I've been involved with CGR India (Council for Green Revolution) — attending their events and contributing to their work in sustainable agriculture across Telangana.

The problem I set out to solve

India's irrigation is broken

Small and marginal farmers in Telangana — those owning less than 2 hectares — irrigate entirely by instinct. They flood their fields on fixed schedules regardless of what the plant needs, the weather, or soil moisture levels.

The result: massive water waste, soil degradation, waterlogging, and paradoxically — lower yields. The very act of trying to help their crops ends up harming them.

I wanted to know if a teenager with sensors and an internet connection could prove a better way — not in a lab, but in an actual field.

Scientific basis

The methodology

ET₀
Reference Evapotranspiration
ET₀ is the rate at which water evaporates from a reference crop under given weather conditions. Calculated using the FAO Penman-Monteith equation, it gives a daily baseline of atmospheric water demand used as PANI's primary input.
ET₀ = f(solar radiation, temp, humidity, wind)
Kc
Crop Coefficients (Kc)
Each crop has a Kc value varying by growth stage (initial, development, mid-season, late). Multiplying ET₀ by the stage-specific Kc gives actual crop water need (ETc). I applied FAO-56 Kc tables calibrated for Telangana conditions.
ETc = ET₀ × Kc(stage)
IoT
Smart Sensor Network
Capacitive soil moisture sensors measured volumetric water content at the root zone every 15 minutes. Readings were cross-referenced with ETc demand to determine whether irrigation was justified — preventing both under- and over-watering.
Irrigate if: VWC < (FC − MAD × (FC − PWP))
AI
AI Decision Layer
A lightweight AI model trained on local field data refined scheduling decisions by learning seasonal patterns and plot-specific response curves. Over time it reduced prediction error and adapted to micro-climate variability that static tables can't capture.
Input: [ET₀, VWC, Kc, DaysSinceLast, Stage]

About CGR India

An NGO working on green futures

CGR India — the Council for Green Revolution — is an environmental NGO based in Hyderabad focused on sustainable agriculture, water conservation, and rural livelihoods. Their work spans field practice, community outreach, and policy advocacy across Telangana.

They're listed on the NSE Social Stock Exchange, which reflects a standard of transparency and accountability that distinguishes them from most environmental organisations of their size.

I got involved with CGR through a shared interest in agricultural technology and water conservation — attending their events, engaging with their research, and contributing where I could.
