Teaching Smart Irrigation

Project Paani

The classroom version of my research: teaching children in Hyderabad how smart, need-based irrigation works and why every drop counts. Built with the Vivekananda Institute of Human Excellence, and designed to keep running on its own.

The idea

Water literacy for the people who'll inherit the fields

The farmers I studied irrigate by habit. Not because they don't care, but because no one ever taught them another way. Project Paani starts a generation earlier.

When children can see for themselves how a plant actually uses water, smart irrigation stops feeling like foreign technology and starts feeling like common sense. The aim isn't to turn them into engineers; it's to make "water only when the plant needs it" the natural way they think about growing anything.

The problem I set out to solve

India's irrigation is broken

Small and marginal farmers in Telangana, those owning less than 2 hectares, irrigate largely by instinct, flooding fields on fixed schedules regardless of what the plant, the weather, or the soil is telling them.

The result: wasted water, soil degradation, waterlogging, and often lower yields. The very act of trying to help the crop ends up hurting it.

I wanted to know whether a learned scheduling policy could beat habit, and whether it could be trained safely, without experimenting on a real season's crop.

Why reinforcement learning

You can't afford to experiment on a real field

Standard online reinforcement learning learns by trial and error: it has to take bad actions to discover they're bad. On a crop, a season of bad waterings means a ruined harvest. That isn't an acceptable way to learn.

So PANI uses offline RL: the agent learns entirely from a fixed dataset of episodes from a soil-water simulator plus real field logs, and never explores on a live plant. The catch is that a naïve offline agent grows overconfident about actions it never actually saw in the data.

Implicit Q-Learning is built to avoid exactly that. It never queries the value of an unseen action, which makes it stable to train from logged data. That is the property that matters most when the cost of a mistake is a farmer's season.

IQL
How Implicit Q-Learning works
IQL estimates a value function V as an upper expectile of the Q-values seen in the data, fits Q by temporal-difference learning against that V, and extracts the policy by advantage-weighted regression, cloning the good actions more heavily than the bad. Because V is learned only over actions that appear in the data, the agent never over-estimates actions it has never tried.
V ← expectile_τ( Q(s,a) )  ·  π ∝ exp( β · (Q(s,a) − V(s)) )
Framing irrigation as a decision problem

The MDP

State
Soil moisture (VWC), ET₀ forecast, crop stage / Kc, days since last irrigation, recent weather.
Action
How much to irrigate, and whether to irrigate at all this step: a volume or duration, or none.
Reward
A yield proxy, minus water used, minus penalties for drought stress and waterlogging.
Data
Episodes from a randomised soil-water simulator, plus real sensor logs from the field.
The physics the agent learns on

The agronomy underneath

ET₀
Reference Evapotranspiration
ET₀ is the rate at which water evaporates from a reference crop under given weather. Computed with the FAO Penman-Monteith equation, it gives a daily baseline of atmospheric water demand, a core feature the agent reads at every step.
ET₀ = f(solar radiation, temp, humidity, wind)
Kc
Crop Coefficients (Kc)
Each crop's water need varies by stage: initial, development, mid-season, late. Multiplying ET₀ by the stage-specific Kc gives crop water need (ETc). FAO-56 Kc tables, calibrated for Telangana, ground the simulator's dynamics.
ETc = ET₀ × Kc(stage)
IoT
Smart Sensor Network
Capacitive soil-moisture sensors measure volumetric water content at the root zone. Their readings both feed the state and generate the real logs used to check that the simulator, and the learned policy, behave sensibly in the field.
Irrigate if: VWC < (FC − MAD × (FC − PWP))
S2R
Sim-to-Real & Baselines
The simulator is domain-randomised across soil types, weather and crop stages so the policy transfers rather than overfits. I compare offline methods head-to-head, CQL (conservative), IQL (implicit) and H2O (dynamics-aware), against classic threshold scheduling.
Baselines: CQL · IQL · H2O · ETc-threshold

The device

Precise watering, without the price tag

Most precision-irrigation systems assume a farmer can afford sensors, controllers, and power. The vast majority of small and marginal farmers can't, which is exactly why the group that needs efficient watering most is the one it reaches least.

The device closes that gap: a simple, low-cost way to deliver water precisely at the root zone, built for farms that can't support sensor-heavy setups. It's the hardware counterpart to the PANI scheduling research. Where the model decides, this is what acts.

Machine Learning · Agri-Markets

Crop price predictor

I built a website to predict crop prices from agricultural market data, so a farmer could decide when to sell. The first model produced a graph where a crop's predicted price spiked one day and collapsed the next, with nothing in the records to explain the swing.

My instinct was to make the algorithm more complex. The real problem was less flattering: the data had gaps, inconsistent reporting intervals, and too few reliable observations for some crops. I tried smoothing the abrupt jumps, changing the variables the model considered, and narrowing predictions to crops with fuller histories. Each fix corrected one distortion and revealed another.

The model is still imperfect, and that is the part that stuck with me.

PythonMLTime-series
What it taught me Computation can't remove uncertainty just because it turns the world into numbers. What it can do is help locate that uncertainty: in the world itself, in how the data was collected, or in an assumption hidden inside the model.
02
Computer Vision · Assistive Tech

Sign language to speech

The idea started in Rajapet, my native village in Telangana, during door-to-door outreach. I greeted a man who didn't respond. He was deaf, and the people around him communicated in basic gestures. I left thinking about how much a person can have to say, and how little gets across without a shared language.

After studying computer-vision models in Brown's "Using AI to Make AI" course, I built a prototype: using an American Sign Language alphabet dataset, it classifies hand shapes and turns the detected letters into speech. Then I noticed everything the demo concealed: it needed a laptop, it was sensitive to lighting, background and hand position, it only went one way, and an ASL alphabet classifier wasn't actually suited to the language or daily life of the man who inspired it.

That gap became the design challenge. I simplified the path from recognition to speech and sketched a concept for pairing the program with camera-equipped glasses, so it could move with the user instead of staying tied to a desk. With two teammates I developed the idea for Negotium, my school's entrepreneurship competition, and we reached the final round.
